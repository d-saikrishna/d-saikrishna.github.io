# Chaduvu | Book Review

As I write this, I have six courses I’ve enrolled in on Swayam. Four on Coursera. And a long list of books sitting in my bucket list.

Every now and then, I ask myself: “What will I really gain by doing all these courses and reading all these books?” Most of the time, I answer myself with the “joy of learning” argument. That isn’t a lie. I genuinely feel a lot of happiness in learning new things. I haven’t found a better pastime than learning. I take great pride in calling myself a lifelong student.

In the novel Chaduvu, the protagonist Sundaram also studies for pleasure. A perpetual student. But a lifelong student without responsibility. Even when his mother falls into debt because of his education, it doesn’t prick him in the slightest. He may sell things at home to survive because he cannot support his family, but he will not stop studying.

Should education be tied to money and employment? The joy-of-learning philosophy stands against that, doesn’t it? Sundaram derives immense happiness from his studies. Isn’t that enough?

It didn’t feel like enough.

This idea of joy is a complicated thing. It’s hard to measure it with a single value. If we measure it only in money, we already see the consequences around us.

But saying money should not be a measure at all is also wrong. Research on happiness tells us one thing: money and happiness have a non-linear relationship. Money is necessary. But beyond a certain point, each additional rupee does not add much happiness. If we can figure out what that threshold is, secure that much, and then focus on other pursuits, we may live more happily.

To put it simply, happiness is like a dish you cook. Money is like salt in it. If you don’t know how much salt to add, the whole dish gets spoiled.

There is another question too. Why couldn’t Sundaram, despite studying so much, earn the money society expects? A central theme in the book is the criticism that our education does not translate into “useful” work. The knowledge required for the occupations pursued by ninety percent of people is not taught in our schools. In fact, it isn’t even recognized as knowledge or skill. That criticism still holds true today.

If Sundaram had been poor, there would have been nothing left to sell. If he had been rich, his education would not have been dismissed as neglect. Because he was middle class, living the way he liked came to be seen as irresponsibility.

There is a Sundaram inside all of us too, isn’t there? A Sundaram who struggles to live the way he wants.
