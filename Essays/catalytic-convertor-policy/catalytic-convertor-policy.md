# How Catalytic Converters entered Indian Environmental Policy?

Catalytic converters are exhaust emission control devices fitted behind tailpipes in our vehicles. They convert toxic gases like carbon-monoxide (CO), Nitrogen Oxides (NOx) and partially burnt fuels to less harmful Nitrogen (N2), Carbon dioxide (CO2) and water (H2O). [Up to 98% of the pollutants are not emitted because of the use of catalytic converters](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cxe81348llpo). However, they are costly ([10,000 to 60,000 rupees](https://www.coverfox.com/car-insurance/articles/catalytic-converter-in-cars/)) as their ceramic honeycomb core is coated with metals like platinum, palladium, rhodium and sometimes gold. So costly that [thieves try to steal it off vehicles](https://www.thehindu.com/news/international/two-18-year-olds-charged-with-murder-of-former-general-hospital-actor-johnny-wactor/article68545701.ece).

High costs are used to justify why pollution control is not prioritised. [High costs of retrofitting powerplants](https://indianexpress.com/article/india/govt-defends-revised-framework-for-so2-emission-norms-10126453/) was used by the Union Government to justify exempting 78% of thermal power plants from installing Flue Gas De-sulphurisation (FGD) systems. Brick kilns find the cost of retrofitting their kilns a hindrance to transition to cleaner technologies like zig-zag kilns. Farmers find labor or happy seeder machines costly and thus burn crop residue.

In this context, how come a costly pollution control intervention device like a catalytic converter became acceptable in India? What policy history did it see? What lessons can other pollution control interventions learn from this history?

## Judicial Intervention

In 1985, environmental lawyer Mahesh Chander Mehta filed a Public Interest Litigation (PIL) in the Supreme Court arguing that air pollution in Delhi violated Right to Life under Article 21. The Supreme Court constituted a committee under the chairmanship of retired Justice K.N Saikia. This committee gave its [recommendations](https://www.transportpolicy.net/wp-content/uploads/2021/08/status-of-the-vehicular-pollution.pdf) in 1991. It is here that catalytic converters made its foray into Indian environmental policy making.  The committee recommended _making it compulsory for all petrol vehicles on the road to retrofit a suitable catalytic converter or a suitable emission control device so as to control CO and HC with effect from 1.4.1992._

Later in 1994, the [Supreme Court ordered that all new vehicles (Petrol driven) should be equipped with catalytic converters](https://www.downtoearth.org.in/environment/catalytic-convertors-essential-27465) by April 1995 in four metro cities (Delhi, Calcutta, Mumbai, Madras). It also ordered that lead free petrol should be made available in all four metros as catalytic converters get damaged when there is lead in petrol. This measure was later extended to all metros, state capitals and Union Territories from 1998.

## Government implementation

Governments took various policy measures to implement the Supreme Court order.

- The Union government [notified under the Motor Vehicle Rules in 1996 mandating catalytic converters in all metros, state capitals and Union Territories from 1998](http://indiankanoon.org/doc/145507593/).
- [Delhi Government came up with a subsidy to install catalytic converters](https://delhiassembly.delhi.gov.in/dlas/23rd-mar-1995). 
- Governments also attempted to be a model user and [installed catalytic converters in government vehicles.](https://elaw.org/resource/india-mc-mehta-v-union-india-wp-130291985-1996-0426-vehicle-pollution-case-government-vehicl?utm_source=chatgpt.com) 

The Government later moved away from selecting a particular technology when it transitioned to Bharat Stage (BS) emission norms in 2000. The Supreme Court ruled that all vehicles in India should meet BS1 norms from 2000. Later in 2005, much more stringent emissions norms BS2 were mandated nationwide. To meet BS2 norms, all petrol vehicles started having catalytic converters and they became effectively mandatory.

## Unintended consequences

The catalytic converters policy created a few unintended consequences.

- Vehicles with catalytic converters need unleaded petrol. When the Supreme Court mandated catalytic converters in four metros in 1996, it also ordered governments to ensure supply of unleaded petrol in four metros. However there was a shortage of unleaded petrol outside the cities and this led to [new cars being useless for long distance travel](https://archive.org/stream/in.ernet.dli.2015.99430/2015.99430.Sunday-Vol22may-september1995_djvu.txt).
- Unleaded petrol was costlier was in short-supply. There were incidents of [people tampering their new cars and removing catalytic converters](https://indiankanoon.org/doc/995571/), making the policy ineffective. The government and courts found their policy wanting to plug these tamperings.

Today, every car in India has a catalytic converter. This piece of pollution control has become a norm and we all bear the costs required to protect air. We don’t lament that automobile sales are reducing because catalytic converters increased vehicle costs. In fact, India produces and [exports](https://www.thehindu.com/news/cities/bangalore/mexico-working-with-india-on-pollution-climate-change/article25692024.ece) catalytic converters, generating ‘green’ jobs. I hope this story inspires other pollution control interventions in becoming a law soon. To pollute is cheap in the short-term, but costly in the long-term.
