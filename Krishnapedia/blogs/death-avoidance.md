# Keeping death in perspective
<div style="text-align: center"><img src="en.wikipedia.org/wiki/When_Breath_Becomes_Air#/media/File:When_Breath_Becomes_Air.jpg" style="width: 100%; max-width: 500px;"/></div>

**“Do you want to know how would you die? Or when you would die?”**

A friend asked me this question. He chose the former and I chose the latter. I felt I can make better use of whatever time I’ll have depending on how much I have. I’ll skip all the delayed gratification, if delay is out of question.

And then I read these words of [Paul Kalanithi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Kalanithi) from his memoir, [“When breath becomes air”](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/When_Breath_Becomes_Air)

> “If I had some sense of how much time I have left, it’d be easier. If I had two years, I’d write. If I had ten, I’d get back to surgery and science.”

Lucy Kalanithi, his wife, wrote in the epilogue that we live in a death-avoidant culture. All our decisions are made in the expectation that we’d live forever despite ample evidence otherwise.  

> But what I desired—life—was not what I was confident about—death.

This book attempts to bring back death to our perspective. And I still believe we can live a richer life keeping death in perspective. 

> even if I’m dying, until I actually die, I am still living.
