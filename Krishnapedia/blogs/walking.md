# Nature walkers! Assemble!

If you identify as a nature walker, you should read this essay _Walking_ by Henry David Thoreau. It was his love for walking in nature, the wild west of the 19th century.

> Two or three hours' walking will carry me to as strange a country as I expect ever to see. A single farmhouse which I had not seen before is sometimes as good as the dominions of the King of Dahomey.

Walking is Indians favourite exercise, but that is not what Thoreau celebrated here. Not finishing some laps. Not logging it on strava. 

He celebrated walking for self-introspection. 
> you must walk like a camel, which is said to be the only beast which ruminates when walking.

He celebrated walking to get lost in nature, in wilderness.
> To preserve wild animals implies generally the creation of a forest for them to dwell in or resort to. So it is with man.

He celebrated wilderness in humans which he feels is awaken by nature walks.
> I would not have every man nor every part of a man cultivated, any more than I would have every acre of earth cultivated: part will be tillage, but the greater part will be meadow and forest, not only serving an immediate use, but preparing a mould against a distant future, by the annual decay of the vegetation which it supports.

Some problems that nature walkers face have been there since his time. He laments how a walker loses freedom as landscapes increasingly become privatised. There’ll be fences and roads. A walker can’t climb a rock. 
> To enjoy a thing exclusively is commonly to exclude yourself from the true enjoyment of it.

He articulated what we all nature walkers feel in unison. 
> Hope and the future for me are not in lawns and cultivated fields, not in towns and cities, but in the impervious and quaking swamps.
