Counter factual is an important thought experiment in statistics. When we want to know if a medicine improves a patient’s condition, we should also check if the condition would have improved had patient was not given the medicine.

Virginia Wolf did a similar thought experiment in ‘A Room of one’s own’. She imagined a counter-factual to Shakespeare — his sister with equal literary gifts as Shakespeare. Would she become as famous as Shakespeare?

    Meanwhile his extraordinarily gifted sister, let us suppose, remained at home. She was as adventurous, as imaginative, as agog to see the world as he was. But she was not sent to school. She had no chance of learning grammar and logic, let alone of reading Horace and Virgil. She picked up a book now and then, one of her brother's perhaps, and read a few pages. But then her parents came in and told her to mend the stockings or mind the stew and not moon about with books and papers.

Woolf also cited a randomised control trial done by dairy company to make case for how differential treatment meted out to women would affect their literary contribution.

    Effect of discouragement upon the mind of the artist should be measured, as I have seen a dairy company measure the effect of ordinary milk and Grade A milk upon the body of the rat. They set two rats in cages side by side, and of the two one was furtive, timid and small, and the other was glossy, bold and big. Now what food do we feed women as artists upon?

Woolf indeed had a statistical mind.
